# Narrative for Diagnostics

> How Growmerce speaks during the diagnostic. The diagnostic is the Intelligence Brain reasoning
> on the visitor's business in public (`04-website-ux/Commerce_Diagnostic_Experience.md`). Its
> language must feel like a sharp operator interviewing you — intelligent, never a quiz, never a
> form, never a lead funnel in disguise.

---

## Objective

Guide the user through giving context and receiving real findings, in language that makes the
intelligence *visible* and builds confidence step by step — so the diagnostic itself is the proof
of expertise.

## Tone

Curious, precise, collaborative, calm. The voice of an analyst who is clearly *using* what you
just told them. Encouraging without being chirpy; honest about limits without being discouraging.

## Language

- **Questions sound like an operator's, not a survey's.** Short, concrete, one idea each, in
  real commerce nouns ("Where do you sell?", "Which items should be your bestsellers?").
- **Narrate the reasoning.** Tell the user what's being examined and why ("we're checking whether
  demand is reaching you before we look at conversion").
- **Show evidence and missing data honestly** as you go ("from your link we can see X; for profit
  we'd need Y").
- **Confidence is spoken as it forms** ("now that you've added order data, we can speak to margin
  with higher confidence").
- **Suggestions are offered, not imposed** ("we think these are your competitors — accept, edit,
  or remove").
- **Findings are recognitions** (19), in the operator's words, each with how-we-know and how-sure.

## Examples

- Context: "Tell us where you sell and what — we'll find where it's leaking." (not "Step 1 of 7:
  Business Information.")
- Working: "Checking discovery vs. demand… separating revenue from profit… ruling out the obvious
  innocent explanations." (honest reasoning, not a fake spinner.)
- Missing data: "We can read your discovery and conversion. To judge whether your discounting is
  actually profitable, we'd need rough order volume — optional, but it unlocks the profit view."
- Finding: "Your traffic arrives but doesn't convert — and the evidence points to a trust gap at
  checkout, not a price problem. Medium confidence; your order data would confirm it."

## Things to avoid

- Quiz/personality-test framing ("What kind of seller are you?").
- Form language ("required field", "Step 3 of 8", "Submit").
- Fake progress or fake certainty ("Analyzing… we found 14 problems!").
- Asking for email before delivering value.
- Inventing findings or numbers when data is thin — abstain honestly instead (GUARD, 20).
- Overwhelm: dumping every possible issue rather than the prioritised few.

> The diagnostic's language should make the user feel they're watching a real intelligence reason
> about their business — and wanting more of it. Every sentence either reveals intelligence or
> reduces friction.
