# Competitor Patterns

> Recurring competitive-dynamics realities. Not "we have competition" — that's a category.
> These are the specific, repeatable ways competitive position is won or lost, regardless of
> who the competitor is or what they sell.

Each pattern uses the canonical template.

---

## Pattern: Acquisition Is Paid While Competitors Compound Organic

**Category:** Competitor

**Description:** The business buys most of its customers while key competitors have built
compounding organic assets — review equity, content footprint, community, marketplace
ranking — that deliver customers at near-zero marginal cost. Every quarter the gap widens:
the competitor's cost-to-acquire falls as the business's rises. It's a race the business is
structurally losing even when its product is better.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "We spend more than them but they always seem to be everywhere."
- Competitor outranks/outsells despite similar or worse product.
- Rising ad costs just to hold position.
- Competitor's content/reviews snowball while ours stay flat.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Seller's paid-revenue share high; competitor's organic visibility high.
- Competitor review velocity and ranking compounding over time.
- Competitor share-of-search rising on category terms; seller's flat.
- Competitor's content/social footprint growing; seller buying every impression.

**Common Root Causes:**
- No organic asset strategy (reviews, content, community, ranking equity).
- Treating acquisition as a media-buying problem only.
- Late start — competitor banked equity earlier and now compounds.
- Performance team rewarded on short-term ROAS, not durable assets.

**Revenue Impact:** Growing structural disadvantage — the visibility ceiling rises for the
competitor and stays flat for the seller.

**Profit Impact:** Severe and widening — seller pays full freight per customer forever while
competitor's blended acquisition cost trends to zero.

**Risk Level:** High — compounding gaps are very expensive to close later.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Time-series showing competitor organic compounding vs. seller's paid dependence.
- Clear share-of-search divergence.
- Review/ranking velocity gap.

**Counter Evidence:**
- Competitor's "organic" is actually heavily funded (then it's a spend fight, not an equity gap).
- Category genuinely has no organic surface (pure paid market).

**Quick Wins:**
- Launch a review-velocity push to start banking the cheapest organic equity.
- Claim and optimise the free organic surfaces (marketplace organic, profiles, basic SEO).
- Repurpose existing customers/content into compounding assets.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Build an organic-asset roadmap (reviews, content, community) with compounding targets.
- Shift budget mix toward asset-building over time.
- Establish ranking equity on the primary platform.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Track blended acquisition cost trend vs. competitor, not just this month's ROAS.
- Standing "organic equity" KPIs alongside paid KPIs.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent tracks competitor organic footprint over time and
projects the acquisition-cost divergence, flagging when the gap crosses a danger threshold.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Competitive Durability module. Triggered by paid dependence + competitor
organic compounding.

**Report Usage:** "They're building assets while you're renting attention — and the gap
compounds." Shown with the paid-vs-organic trajectory chart.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Organic-equity build track running alongside paid management.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Competitive-equity monitor projecting the long-run acquisition-cost gap.

## Pattern: Competing On Price Against An Opponent With A Structural Cost Advantage

**Category:** Competitor

**Description:** The business is in a price fight with a competitor who can sustainably sell
lower because of a real structural advantage (scale, vertical integration, platform
subsidy, private label). Every price match erodes the business's margin while barely
denting the competitor's. The business is fighting on the one axis where it cannot win.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "We keep matching their prices and it's killing us."
- Margin shrinks with every competitive response.
- Customers cite the competitor's price as the reason for leaving.
- Feels like a race to the bottom with no floor.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Competitor consistently prices below the seller's cost-plus-target floor and sustains it.
- Seller's margin compresses in periods of price matching.
- Competitor has visible scale/integration/subsidy advantages.
- Price is the dominant cited reason in lost-deal/churn data.

**Common Root Causes:**
- Default to price competition instead of differentiation.
- No value narrative to justify a premium.
- Misreading a structural cost gap as a tactical one.
- Fear of losing price-sensitive customers who were never profitable.

**Revenue Impact:** Price matching may hold volume briefly but caps it — and concedes the
framing to the opponent's strength.

**Profit Impact:** Severe — matching a structurally cheaper rival transfers your margin to
the customer with no durable share gain.

**Risk Level:** High — sustained price wars against a cost-advantaged rival end one way.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Evidence the competitor's low price is structurally sustainable.
- Margin erosion correlated with matching behaviour.
- Differentiation potential that's currently unused.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The seller actually has the cost advantage (then price competition is fine).
- The price gap is a temporary promo, not structural.

**Quick Wins:**
- Stop matching on the items where the gap is structural; redirect to differentiated value.
- Surface the non-price reasons customers choose you (service, speed, quality, bundle).
- Segment out price-only customers who never had healthy economics.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Reposition onto an axis you can win (value, experience, niche, bundle, service).
- Build a value narrative that justifies the price (see `Pricing_Patterns.md`).
- Choose the segments where structural cost is *not* the deciding factor.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Competitive-response policy that forbids reflexive price matching on structural gaps.
- Win/loss tracking by *reason*, not just outcome.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent classifies whether a competitor's price gap is
structural or tactical and recommends differentiate-vs-match per SKU/segment.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Competitive Position module. Triggered by margin erosion + structural
competitor cost advantage.

**Report Usage:** "You're fighting on the one battlefield you can't win." Shown with the
margin-erosion-from-matching chart and a differentiation map.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Repositioning sprint: value narrative, segment selection, offer
redesign.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Competitive-pricing intelligence that flags structural vs. tactical
gaps and recommends response.

## Pattern: The Category Leader Owns The Comparison Frame

**Category:** Competitor

**Description:** A dominant competitor has defined the criteria buyers use to compare options
("everyone compares on X spec / X feature / X price-per-unit"), and those criteria favour the
leader. The business competes inside a frame designed for it to lose, even when it's superior
on dimensions the buyer never gets prompted to consider.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "Customers always ask about the one thing the market leader is best at."
- Sales conversations forced onto the competitor's terms.
- Our genuine advantages never come up in the buyer's evaluation.
- We feel like the "alternative to" rather than a choice in our own right.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Buyer questions/comparisons cluster on the leader-favouring criteria.
- Reviews and content in the category all use the leader's framing.
- Seller's differentiated strengths absent from buyer evaluation language.
- Marketplace filters/specs reflect the leader's dimensions.

**Common Root Causes:**
- Accepting the incumbent's comparison frame by default.
- No deliberate effort to introduce alternative buying criteria.
- Marketing that reacts to the leader instead of reframing the choice.

**Revenue Impact:** Caps share — the seller can only ever be "the cheaper/other version of
the leader," limiting both volume and price.

**Profit Impact:** Forces price competition (see prior pattern), compressing margin.

**Risk Level:** Medium-High — strategic ceiling rather than acute bleed, but very sticky.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Consistent buyer language centred on leader-favouring criteria.
- Demonstrable seller strengths the frame ignores.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The dominant criteria genuinely are what matters most to buyers (reframing would be dishonest).
- The seller has no real differentiated strength to elevate.

**Quick Wins:**
- Introduce one new comparison criterion the seller wins on, in listings/sales scripts.
- Add content that teaches buyers a better way to evaluate the category.
- Reframe the hero message around the buyer's deeper job-to-be-done, not the leader's spec.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Deliberately establish alternative buying criteria the seller owns.
- Build category-education content that shifts the frame over time.
- Anchor positioning on an axis the leader can't easily claim.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Sales/marketing playbooks that lead with the reframe, never the leader's terms.
- Win/loss analysis tracking which frame the buyer used.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent analyses buyer language and category content to detect
which comparison frame dominates and where the seller could introduce a winning criterion.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Positioning module. Triggered when buyer evaluation language is
dominated by leader-favouring criteria.

**Report Usage:** "You're playing by your competitor's rules." Shown with a comparison-frame
map and the unused-strengths gap.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Positioning/messaging sprint to introduce and seed new buying criteria.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Frame-analysis tool mining reviews/content to surface reframing
opportunities.
