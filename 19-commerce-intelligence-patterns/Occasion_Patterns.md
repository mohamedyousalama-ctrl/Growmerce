# Occasion Patterns

> Seasonal and occasion-driven demand realities. Not "sell more at holidays" — these are the
> recurring ways businesses mishandle the predictable rhythm of demand around peaks,
> occasions, and seasons.

Each pattern uses the canonical template.

---

## Pattern: Preparing For The Peak During The Peak

**Category:** Occasion

**Description:** The business scrambles to get ready for a predictable demand event (a sale
season, a holiday, a gifting occasion) only once it has already begun — building creative,
stock, and offers in the window when it should be executing them. The most profitable days of
the year are spent setting up instead of selling, and the early-bird demand (often the
highest-intent) is missed entirely.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "Every peak season feels like chaos."
- Stock arrives mid-peak; campaigns launch after demand has started.
- Early high-intent shoppers find the business unprepared.
- The team burns out and still leaves money on the table.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Campaign/launch timing lagging the demand curve's onset.
- Stockouts on occasion-relevant SKUs during the peak.
- Lower share of early-window sales vs. competitors.
- Repeating year-over-year scramble with no pre-built playbook.

**Common Root Causes:**
- No occasion calendar with lead-time-aware preparation.
- Treating recurring events as surprises.
- Stock/creative lead times not back-planned from the demand onset.
- Reactive rather than scheduled occasion operations.

**Revenue Impact:** High — the early, high-intent portion of the peak is the most valuable and
the most often missed.

**Profit Impact:** Mixed — missed full-price early demand often gets recovered later only via
discounting, compressing margin.

**Risk Level:** Medium-High — predictable and entirely preventable, which makes the loss
especially avoidable.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Timing lag documented against the demand curve.
- Repeating annual pattern.
- Occasion-SKU stockouts during peak.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The category genuinely has unpredictable demand spikes (not calendar-driven).
- The business deliberately enters late for a clearance/long-tail strategy.

**Quick Wins:**
- Build the next-occasion calendar now, back-planned from demand onset and lead times.
- Pre-stage creative and offers for the next event so launch is a switch, not a build.
- Secure occasion-SKU stock ahead of the lead-time deadline.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Standing annual occasion calendar with preparation deadlines per event.
- Demand-curve-aware stock and creative planning.
- Capture early-window demand at full price before any discounting.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Occasion playbooks (18) triggered on a lead-time schedule, not on the date itself.
- Post-occasion retro feeding next year's plan.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent maps historical demand curves to upcoming occasions
and alerts when preparation deadlines (stock, creative, offers) are approaching unmet.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Occasion Readiness module. Triggered by timing lag + occasion-SKU
stockouts.

**Report Usage:** "You're setting up for the rush after it's already started." Shown with
the launch-timing-vs-demand-curve overlay.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Occasion-operations build: calendar, back-planned deadlines,
pre-staged assets.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Occasion-readiness scheduler with deadline alerts driven by demand curves.

## Pattern: Treating A Gifting Occasion Like A Self-Purchase Occasion

**Category:** Occasion

**Description:** During gifting peaks, the *buyer* and the *user* are different people with
different needs, but the business markets, merchandises, and supports as if the buyer were
buying for themselves. Gifters need confidence (will they like it?), convenience (gift
wrap, delivery timing, easy choice) and reassurance (easy returns/exchange) — none of which
the self-purchase journey provides. The occasion is misread.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "We do okay at gifting season but never as well as we should."
- Gift shoppers hesitate or bounce.
- Lots of "is this a good gift?" questions.
- No gifting-specific experience at all.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Spike in gift-intent search/queries with no matching gifting experience.
- Higher hesitation/abandonment during gifting windows.
- Absence of gift-enabling features (wrap, gift messaging, guided choice, easy exchange).
- Reviews/queries mentioning gifting use the product wasn't merchandised for.

**Common Root Causes:**
- Journey built only for the self-purchaser.
- No gift-buyer reassurance (will the recipient like it / be able to exchange it).
- Missing gifting conveniences (wrap, delivery-by-date, gift receipts).
- No guided/curated selection for uncertain gifters.

**Revenue Impact:** High during gifting peaks — gift buyers are abundant and motivated but
easily lost to friction.

**Profit Impact:** Favourable — gifting often skews to full price and higher AOV when the
experience supports it.

**Risk Level:** Medium — concentrated in occasion windows but highly recoverable.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Gift-intent signals without a matching experience.
- Gifting-window-specific hesitation.
- Missing gift features competitors offer.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The category isn't gifted (genuine self-purchase product).
- A gifting experience already exists and performs.

**Quick Wins:**
- Add gift essentials before the next gifting peak: gift messaging, delivery-by-date, easy exchange.
- Create a guided "perfect gift" selection for uncertain buyers.
- Add reassurance copy: "easy to exchange if it's not quite right."

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Build a distinct gifting journey for gifting occasions.
- Merchandise gift-appropriate bundles and price points.
- Support the buyer-≠-user reality end to end (purchase → delivery → exchange).

**Operational Fixes:**
- Gifting-experience checklist activated on the occasion calendar.
- Gift-intent capture feeding merchandising.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent detects gift-intent signals and audits whether the
journey offers gifting affordances, recommending the missing ones ahead of the occasion.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Occasion Experience module. Triggered by gift-intent signals + missing
gifting experience.

**Report Usage:** "Your gift shoppers are being treated like they're shopping for themselves."
Shown with gift-intent vs. gifting-feature presence.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Gifting-experience build ahead of the relevant occasion.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Gift-readiness audit triggered by occasion calendar and intent signals.

## Pattern: Post-Peak Cliff With No Plan To Convert The Surge Into A Base

**Category:** Occasion

**Description:** The business wins a flood of new customers during a peak, then lets them
vanish — no plan to convert occasion buyers into repeat customers. Every peak resets to zero
instead of compounding a base, so the business rents a seasonal spike each year rather than
banking a growing customer foundation. The hardest, most expensive customers to win are
acquired and then dropped.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "We get a huge spike then it goes dead."
- New-customer surge in the peak, almost no repeat after.
- Each year starts from scratch despite years of peaks.
- No post-peak engagement plan.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Large new-customer cohort in the peak with near-zero second purchase.
- No post-peak lifecycle/winback for occasion cohorts.
- Flat baseline outside peaks despite years of peak acquisition.
- Owned-audience growth not tracking peak acquisition volume.

**Common Root Causes:**
- Treating peak as a sales event, not an acquisition-and-retention event.
- No post-peak onboarding/winback for the surge cohort.
- Occasion buyers not captured into owned audience.
- Retention seen as out-of-season work.

**Revenue Impact:** Very high cumulatively — converting even a fraction of each peak cohort
into repeat buyers compounds the baseline year over year.

**Profit Impact:** Severe opportunity cost — the surge was acquired at peak-competition cost,
then its lifetime value is forfeited.

**Risk Level:** Medium-High — chronic value leakage disguised as seasonality.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Peak cohort second-purchase rate near zero.
- Flat post-peak baseline across years.
- Absent post-peak lifecycle.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The occasion product is inherently one-time/annual (then focus on annual reactivation, not
  near-term repeat).
- Peak cohorts do repeat but via an under-attributed channel.

**Quick Wins:**
- Capture every peak buyer into the owned audience this season.
- Launch a post-peak welcome/second-purchase flow for the surge cohort.
- Schedule an off-peak reactivation touch for occasion buyers.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Treat each peak as base-building: acquisition + retention plan together.
- Build occasion-cohort lifecycle journeys (welcome, value, reorder, reactivation).
- Set an owned-audience growth target tied to peak volume.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Peak-cohort retention rate as a tracked KPI.
- Post-peak retention plan baked into every occasion playbook.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent tracks peak-cohort repeat behaviour and flags surges
that aren't being converted into a base, recommending lifecycle interventions.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Occasion-to-Retention module. Triggered by high peak acquisition + low
peak-cohort repeat.

**Report Usage:** "You win an army of customers every peak and then lose them all." Shown
with the peak-cohort decay curve across years.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Peak-cohort retention build: capture, welcome, reactivation.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Occasion-cohort retention tracker with intervention triggers.
