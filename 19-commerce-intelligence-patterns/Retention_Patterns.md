# Retention Patterns

> Why customers do or don't come back. Not "bad retention" — these are the recurring
> mechanisms behind churn and loyalty, each diagnosable and fixable.

Each pattern uses the canonical template.

---

## Pattern: Churn Happens At A Predictable Moment Nobody Is Watching

**Category:** Retention

**Description:** Customers churn at a specific, recurring point in their lifecycle — right
after the first order's "honeymoon," at the natural reorder deadline, after a price increase,
after a service failure — and that moment is entirely predictable, but no one is watching it
or intervening. Churn looks random and unavoidable; it's actually clustered and catchable.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "Customers just drift away and we don't know why."
- Churn feels random and out of the business's control.
- No early warning before a customer goes quiet.
- Reactivation attempts (if any) come far too late.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Churn clustering at a specific lifecycle point (post-first-order, missed reorder window, etc.).
- A measurable "quiet period" preceding churn (declining engagement/orders).
- No intervention triggered at the predictable churn moment.
- Cohort survival curves with a consistent drop-off point.

**Common Root Causes:**
- No lifecycle monitoring or churn-risk signal.
- Missing intervention at the known churn moment.
- Treating churn as inevitable rather than predictable.
- No reorder-window or engagement-decay trigger.

**Revenue Impact:** High — intercepting predictable churn directly protects recurring revenue
and lifetime value.

**Profit Impact:** High and favourable — retaining an existing customer is far cheaper than
acquiring a replacement.

**Risk Level:** High — chronic, compounding loss of the most valuable (existing) customers.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Churn clustering at an identifiable moment.
- A detectable pre-churn signal.
- Cohort curves showing the consistent drop point.

**Counter Evidence:**
- Churn is genuinely random with no detectable signal or cluster.
- The category is inherently one-time (no retention to protect).

**Quick Wins:**
- Identify the churn moment from cohort data and add one intervention there.
- Trigger a reorder/check-in touch at the natural reorder deadline.
- Set up a simple engagement-decay alert for at-risk customers.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Build a churn-risk model and lifecycle intervention programme.
- Design retention touchpoints at each known risk moment.
- Address the root cause of the churn moment (onboarding gap, price shock, service failure).

**Operational Fixes:**
- Churn-moment monitoring as standing practice.
- Intervention triggers wired to lifecycle stages.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent detects the churn-cluster moment, builds a pre-churn
risk signal, and triggers interventions before the customer goes silent.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Churn-Timing module. Triggered by churn clustering + absent intervention.

**Report Usage:** "Your customers leave at a moment you could see coming." Shown with the
cohort survival curve and the churn-cluster point.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Retention-programme build: churn signal, lifecycle interventions.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Churn-prediction engine with automated intervention triggers.

## Pattern: Loyalty Is Assumed From Frequency That's Actually Just Habit Or Lock-In

**Category:** Retention

**Description:** The business reads repeat purchasing as loyalty when it's really habit,
convenience, or switching friction — fragile attachment that evaporates the moment a
competitor removes the friction or offers a better deal. The business under-invests in real
loyalty because the metrics look loyal, then is blindsided when "loyal" customers leave
instantly for an alternative.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "Our customers are loyal" — said without evidence of genuine preference.
- Sudden defection of supposedly loyal customers to a new competitor.
- Repeat purchase with low emotional engagement (no advocacy, no referrals).
- Retention that collapses when friction is removed elsewhere.

**Evidence Signals:**
- High repeat rate but low advocacy (NPS, referrals, reviews).
- Repeat driven by convenience/lock-in, not preference.
- Rapid churn when a friction-removing competitor appears.
- No emotional/relationship signals behind the frequency.

**Common Root Causes:**
- Mistaking transactional frequency for loyalty.
- Relying on switching friction instead of building preference.
- No relationship or advocacy programme.
- Not measuring the *why* behind repeat behaviour.

**Revenue Impact:** Hidden risk — current revenue looks safe but is exposed to sudden
competitive defection.

**Profit Impact:** Deferred — under-investment in real loyalty leaves the base vulnerable and
acquisition-dependent later.

**Risk Level:** High — the danger is precisely that it looks safe.

**Confidence Factors:**
- High repeat + low advocacy divergence.
- Repeat explained by friction/convenience.
- Defection history when friction was removed.

**Counter Evidence:**
- Repeat is backed by genuine advocacy and preference (real loyalty).
- Switching friction is a deliberate, defensible moat.

**Quick Wins:**
- Measure advocacy (referrals, NPS, reviews), not just repeat rate.
- Identify whether repeat is preference or friction-driven.
- Start one relationship-building touch beyond the transaction.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Build genuine preference (brand, experience, community) beneath the frequency.
- Develop an advocacy/referral programme.
- Reduce reliance on lock-in as the retention mechanism.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Track loyalty quality (advocacy), not just loyalty quantity (repeat).
- Periodic "why do they repeat?" research.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent separates habit/lock-in repeat from preference-driven
repeat using advocacy and behaviour signals, flagging fragile "loyalty."

**Diagnostic Usage:** Loyalty-Quality module. Triggered by high repeat + low advocacy.

**Report Usage:** "Your loyal customers aren't loyal — they're stuck, and that won't last."
Shown with repeat-vs-advocacy divergence.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Loyalty-depth sprint: advocacy programme, preference building.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Loyalty-quality score separating preference from friction-driven repeat.

## Pattern: The Best Customers Are Treated Exactly Like The Worst

**Category:** Retention

**Description:** High-value, high-frequency customers receive the same experience, offers, and
attention as one-time bargain hunters — same generic emails, same discounts to people who'd
pay full price, no recognition of their value. The business fails to protect and grow its most
profitable relationships while spending equally on its least profitable ones. Undifferentiated
treatment under-serves the customers who matter most.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "We treat all our customers the same" — said as if it were a virtue.
- Top customers get the same generic marketing as everyone else.
- Discounts going to customers who'd happily pay full price.
- No VIP recognition, no tailored attention for the best.

**Evidence Signals:**
- No segmentation by value/frequency in marketing or service.
- High-value customers receiving generic, often discount-led, messaging.
- No differentiated experience/recognition for top tiers.
- Best customers churning at the same rate as casual ones.

**Common Root Causes:**
- No customer-value segmentation.
- One-size-fits-all communication.
- Discounting indiscriminately (also a Profit Leak pattern).
- Not identifying or protecting the high-value base.

**Revenue Impact:** High — the top customer decile typically drives a disproportionate share
of revenue; protecting and growing them compounds.

**Profit Impact:** High — high-value customers are the most profitable; under-serving them and
discounting to them is doubly costly.

**Risk Level:** Medium-High — quiet erosion of the most valuable relationships.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Absence of value segmentation.
- Generic/discount messaging hitting high-value customers.
- Top-decile churn parity with casual buyers.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The base is genuinely homogeneous in value (rare).
- Differentiated treatment already exists and performs.

**Quick Wins:**
- Identify the top customer decile by value this week.
- Stop sending them discounts they don't need; send recognition instead.
- Give the best customers one differentiated touch (early access, thank-you, VIP perk).

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Build value-based segmentation across marketing and service.
- Create a VIP/high-value programme protecting and growing the top tier.
- Tailor offers and experience to customer value.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Value segmentation as a standing part of CRM.
- Differentiated treatment rules by tier.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent segments customers by value and audits whether
treatment differs, flagging high-value customers receiving low-value (discount-led) experiences.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Value-Segmentation module. Triggered by absent segmentation + generic
high-value treatment.

**Report Usage:** "Your best customers get treated like your worst." Shown with the
revenue-concentration-by-decile chart vs. undifferentiated treatment.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Segmentation + VIP-programme build.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Value-segmentation and VIP-treatment engine.
