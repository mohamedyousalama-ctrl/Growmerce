# Pricing Patterns

> How price is set, framed, and perceived. Not "prices too high/low" — these are the
> recurring ways pricing destroys or leaves value on the table even when the number itself
> is defensible.

Each pattern uses the canonical template.

---

## Pattern: Price Advantage Exists But Value Communication Fails

**Category:** Pricing

**Description:** The business genuinely offers more value at the price than competitors —
better quality, more inclusions, lower true cost of ownership — but never communicates it, so
buyers perceive parity or worse and choose on price alone. The advantage is real and
invisible. Customers can't pay for value they can't see.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "We're actually better value but customers don't get it."
- Losing to competitors who are objectively inferior at the price.
- Sales force/listings can't articulate why the price is worth it.
- Discounting to compete despite already being the better deal.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Objective value advantage (specs, inclusions, durability, service) vs. price-comparable competitors.
- Buyer language treating the category as a commodity / price-only choice.
- Listings/pages leading with price, not value.
- Win/loss citing price when value would have won the argument.

**Common Root Causes:**
- No value narrative; features listed, benefits and value never translated.
- Price shown without the value context that justifies it.
- Assuming buyers will "figure out" the value.
- Sales/marketing not equipped to articulate the advantage.

**Revenue Impact:** High — unlocking perceived value lifts both conversion and the price the
market will bear.

**Profit Impact:** High and favourable — value communication supports price, the opposite of
discounting.

**Risk Level:** Medium-High — leaves the business stuck in price competition it should win.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Demonstrable, objective value advantage.
- Commodity buyer framing despite that advantage.
- Value absent from current messaging.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The value advantage is marginal/subjective (then it's a real parity situation).
- Buyers do perceive the value and still choose price (different, price-elasticity pattern).

**Quick Wins:**
- Add a value-justification block at the price point (what you get that others don't).
- Translate the top 3 features into buyer value on the listing/page.
- Equip sales with a one-line value-vs-price argument.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Build a full value narrative and propagate it across every surface.
- Reframe the comparison onto value, not price (see `Competitor_Patterns.md`).
- Train/enable the team to sell value.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Value-communication standard on every listing/page.
- Win/loss tracking of whether value was communicated.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent compares objective value attributes vs. price-peers
and audits whether messaging communicates that advantage, flagging the gap.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Value-Communication module. Triggered by objective value advantage +
commodity buyer framing.

**Report Usage:** "You're the better deal and nobody knows it." Shown with a value-vs-price
map against competitors.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Value-messaging sprint: narrative, listing/page rewrites, sales enablement.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Value-communication audit comparing attributes to messaging coverage.

## Pattern: Anchorless Pricing (No Reference Point To Judge The Price Against)

**Category:** Pricing

**Description:** The price is presented in isolation with no anchor — no "compare at," no
good/better/best, no cost-of-alternative — so the buyer has nothing to judge it against and
defaults to "expensive." Humans evaluate price relatively, not absolutely; without a
reference the business's price always feels high, regardless of the number.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "People say we're expensive but we're really not."
- Price objections that don't match the actual market position.
- Single price shown with no context or options.
- Buyers can't tell if it's a good deal.

**Evidence Signals:**
- Prices presented standalone, no anchor or tiering.
- "Too expensive" objections out of line with competitive reality.
- No good/better/best or compare-at framing.
- Flat single-option offer structure.

**Common Root Causes:**
- No deliberate price architecture (anchors, tiers, decoys).
- Fear that showing options/anchors looks manipulative.
- Single SKU/price with no context.
- Not understanding relative price perception.

**Revenue Impact:** Medium-High — anchoring and tiering lift both conversion and average
order value.

**Profit Impact:** Favourable — good price architecture moves buyers up tiers and supports premium.

**Risk Level:** Medium — leaves value on the table rather than causing acute loss.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Standalone pricing with no reference.
- Price objections inconsistent with actual market position.
- No tiering where the category supports it.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The category genuinely is single-price/no-anchor by norm and buyers price it accurately.
- Anchoring would be dishonest (no real higher reference exists).

**Quick Wins:**
- Add an honest anchor/reference point at the price (compare-at, cost-of-alternative).
- Introduce a good/better/best where the range supports it.
- Frame the price against the value or the cost of not buying.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Design a deliberate price architecture (tiers, anchors, bundles).
- Build the higher reference point into the range.
- Use decoy/tier structure to guide choice.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Price-presentation standard including a reference point.
- Test anchors/tiers as part of pricing reviews.

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent detects standalone, anchorless pricing and recommends
honest reference points and tiering based on the range.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Price-Architecture module. Triggered by standalone pricing + misaligned
price objections.

**Report Usage:** "Your price has nothing to be judged against, so it always feels high."
Shown with a before/after price-framing mock.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Price-architecture sprint: anchors, tiering, presentation.

**Future SaaS Usage:** Price-presentation analyser recommending anchors and tiers.

## Pattern: One Price For Customers With Very Different Willingness To Pay

**Category:** Pricing

**Description:** The business charges a single price to a customer base with a wide spread of
willingness to pay, leaving money on the table at the top (under-charging buyers who'd happily
pay more for more) and losing volume at the bottom (over-charging price-sensitive buyers who'd
buy a cheaper option). A single price is a compromise that serves nobody and captures the
least value.

**Business Symptoms:**
- "Some customers clearly want premium, others just want the cheapest option."
- Losing budget buyers and under-monetising premium ones simultaneously.
- One product/price for a clearly mixed audience.
- Requests for both "a cheaper version" and "something more premium."

**Evidence Signals:**
- Wide willingness-to-pay spread in the customer base.
- Lost low-end volume and absent high-end capture.
- Single offer/price for a segmented audience.
- Demand signals for both cheaper and premium options.

**Common Root Causes:**
- No good/better/best or tiered offer structure.
- Fear of complexity / cannibalisation.
- Treating a heterogeneous market as homogeneous.
- No segmentation of willingness to pay.

**Revenue Impact:** High — tiering captures both the premium upside and the budget volume.

**Profit Impact:** Favourable — premium tier lifts margin; entry tier expands the base without
discounting the core.

**Risk Level:** Medium — opportunity cost more than acute loss.

**Confidence Factors:**
- Evidence of WTP spread.
- Demand for both cheaper and premium options.
- Single-tier structure today.

**Counter Evidence:**
- The market genuinely is homogeneous in WTP.
- Tiering would fragment a deliberately simple, high-converting single offer.

**Quick Wins:**
- Add a premium tier (more value, higher price) to capture the high-WTP segment.
- Add an honest entry option for price-sensitive buyers.
- Frame the core offer as the "best value" middle.

**Strategic Fixes:**
- Design a good/better/best ladder mapped to WTP segments.
- Build premium inclusions worth a higher price.
- Create an entry tier that expands volume without discounting the core.

**Operational Fixes:**
- Segment-level WTP analysis feeding offer design.
- Tier-mix monitoring (are buyers distributing as intended?).

**AI Detection Opportunities:** An agent detects WTP spread from purchase/behaviour data and
recommends a tier structure to capture both ends.

**Diagnostic Usage:** Segmentation & Tiering module. Triggered by WTP spread + single-tier offer.

**Report Usage:** "One price is leaving money on the table at the top and losing customers at
the bottom." Shown with a WTP-distribution chart and proposed tiers.

**Service Delivery Usage:** Offer-tiering sprint: good/better/best design and rollout.

**Future SaaS Usage:** WTP-segmentation engine recommending tier structures.
