# Arabic Content UX

> The website language is **Arabic Fusha** — practical, clear, business Arabic. Language is not a
> translation layer over an English site; it is the medium in which trust is built. Every word
> must read like a competent commerce operator speaking, not a translated marketing deck. The
> site is **RTL-first** and composed natively in Arabic.

---

## 1. The standard: operator Fusha, not marketing Fusha

The target is **Fusha that an educated commerce decision-maker reads as clear, credible, and
respectful of their intelligence** — modern standard Arabic, businesslike, concrete. Not the
over-formal, ornate Fusha of formal literature, and not weak colloquial-marketing filler.

```
❌ Over-formal / literary     → feels stiff, distant, bureaucratic
❌ Marketing cliché Fusha      → "حلول مبتكرة لنمو استثنائي" (empty, distrusted)
❌ English-structure-translated → unnatural word order, calques, awkward
✅ Operator Fusha              → clear, specific, concrete commerce language
```

---

## 2. The cardinal rule: compose in Arabic, don't translate into it

The whole site is **written natively in Arabic**, structured for the Arabic reader, RTL from the
ground up. English-first copy translated into Arabic produces unnatural structure, calqued idioms,
and a subtly foreign feel that erodes trust with this audience. Where this workstream shows
English phrasings, they are *intent*, to be **composed**, not literally translated.

---

## 3. What to avoid (the trust-killers)

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| Over-formal / literary Fusha | feels distant and bureaucratic; not how operators talk |
| Weak / literal translations | unnatural, low-credibility |
| Marketing clichés ("حلول مبتكرة", "نمو استثنائي") | empty slogans this audience distrusts |
| Vague slogans | no recognition, no specificity |
| English-first structure | awkward Arabic, foreign feel |
| Hype / fake urgency | instantly distrusted |
| Excessive jargon or transliteration where Arabic exists | alienating or sloppy |

---

## 4. Arabic UX rules by text surface

### Headings (عناوين)
- Concrete and operator-grade; name the outcome or the leak, not a slogan.
- Short, scannable, RTL-natural. Lead with the meaning, not a buzzword.
- Recognition headings use the visitor's **felt language** (e.g. naming "تسرّب المبيعات" / "هامش
  الربح" plainly).

### CTAs (أزرار الإجراء)
- Specific and action-led: what happens when they tap, in plain Fusha.
- Example intent: "شخّص تسرّب مبيعاتك" (Diagnose your sales leaks) over a generic "ابدأ الآن";
  "تواصل عبر واتساب وسنراجع نتائجك على أرقامك الحقيقية" over "احجز استشارتك المجانية".
- Warm, peer-to-peer tone — a competent colleague, not a salesperson.

### Diagnostic questions (أسئلة التشخيص)
- Phrased the way a sharp operator would ask: short, concrete, one idea each.
- Use real commerce nouns (السوق/المنصّة، المنيو، عربة الشراء، الطلبات، الهامش) not abstractions.
- Offer chips/options in clear Fusha so the visitor taps, rarely types.

### Error & system messages (رسائل الخطأ والنظام)
- Plain, calm, non-blaming. Say what happened and the next step.
- Honest about limits ("لا نملك بيانات كافية لتقدير الربحية الآن — يمكنك إضافة بيانات التكلفة
  لاحقًا") — mirrors the Brain's honesty.
- Never technical jargon or raw error codes to the user.

### Confidence labels (مستويات الثقة)
- The four bands in clear Fusha: e.g. منخفضة / متوسطة / عالية / عالية جدًا, paired with a plain
  reason ("نرجّح بقوة… ويمكن تأكيدها ببيانات طلباتك").
- The honesty wording is load-bearing for trust — it must read as calibrated, not hedgy or
  wishy-washy.

### Report language (لغة التقرير)
- Executive and decision-oriented: finding, evidence, impact, priority, action — each in tight,
  concrete Fusha.
- Separate revenue (الإيرادات/المبيعات) from profit (الربح/الهامش) explicitly and clearly.
- No dashboard jargon; read like a sharp memo to a decision-maker.

### WhatsApp / consultation text (نصوص واتساب والتواصل)
- Warm, specific, low-pressure; frame the conversation's value ("سنراجع النتائج على أرقامك
  الحقيقية ونخبرك بأهم إصلاحين").
- Pre-filled WhatsApp messages composed in natural Fusha carrying the visitor's context.

## 5. Tone principles (across all Arabic copy)

- **Clarity over eloquence.** The best sentence is the one understood instantly.
- **Specificity over slogans.** Name the real thing (a leak, a margin, a menu) every time.
- **Respect the reader.** No hype, no condescension, no manipulation — operators detect all three.
- **Operator register.** Businesslike peer, not formal authority, not breathless marketer.
- **Consistency of terms.** Fix a clear Arabic vocabulary for core concepts (تسرّب المبيعات،
  تسرّب الربح، الأنماط، التشخيص، عمليات النمو) and use it consistently sitewide.

---

## 6. RTL & bilingual-reality details

- **RTL-first layout** in all intent: reading order, input fields, chips, progress, navigation.
- **Numerals, platform names, and metrics:** platform/brand names (Amazon, Noon, Talabat, Salla,
  Shopify, TikTok…) commonly appear in Latin script; integrate them cleanly within RTL Arabic
  without awkward switching. Keep numbers/units legible in context.
- **Mixed content:** where a Latin term is unavoidable, place it naturally; never transliterate
  clumsily or force Arabic where the operator expects the platform's own name.

---

## 7. A shared terminology spine (to be finalised)

Core concepts need one agreed Arabic term each, used everywhere (homepage, diagnostic, report,
tools). Indicative set (to be locked with a native commerce writer — see Open Questions):
- Sales leak → تسرّب المبيعات
- Profit leak → تسرّب الربح / تآكل الهامش
- Pattern → نمط (الأنماط التجارية)
- Diagnostic → التشخيص
- Confidence → مستوى الثقة
- Growth Operations → عمليات نمو المبيعات
- Recommendation → التوصية / الإجراء المقترح

Consistency here is a trust multiplier; drift (three words for "leak") signals carelessness.

---

## 8. User-State Transition (this surface)

| Lens | Arabic Content UX |
|---|---|
| **State entering** | any state — language is the medium of every transition |
| **State leaving** | the same state, successfully advanced (or, if language fails, regressed) |
| **Friction** | unnatural/over-formal/cliché Arabic that signals "foreign agency / not serious" → removed by native operator Fusha |
| **Trust gained** | credibility through clear, specific, operator-grade Arabic (a continuous trust input, esp. State 5) |
| **Intelligence revealed** | the *register* itself reveals competence; honest confidence/error wording reveals calibration |
| **Next action** | every CTA's Arabic makes the next step clear and specific |

---

## 9. Quality test

Read any sentence aloud to an experienced Arab commerce operator. If it sounds like a smart
colleague explaining their business, it passes. If it sounds like a brochure, a bureaucrat, or a
translation, rewrite it. The Arabic *is* the product's voice — it must sound like the Intelligence
Brain speaking plainly, not like marketing.
