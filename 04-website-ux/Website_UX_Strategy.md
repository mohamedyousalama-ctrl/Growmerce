# Website UX Strategy

> The strategy beneath every page: who the visitor is, what they feel, what stops them, and the
> experience principles that turn intelligence into conversion. Read this before any page
> document — every surface is an application of this strategy.

---

## 0. The organizing principle: states, not pages

This workstream is documented **primarily around user states and cognitive transitions, not
around a page inventory.** The visitor moves through seven states of progressive certainty —
**Curiosity → Recognition → Diagnosis → Evidence → Trust → Action → Commitment** — and pages are
merely *containers* that earn the transition from one state to the next. The full model is in
`User_State_Journey.md`; every surface document carries a **User-State Transition block**. The
conversion thesis below is the *flow of value* that drives those state transitions.

> Why this matters: Claude Design will design around psychology, Claude Code will build flows,
> and the AI agents will know what intelligence to reveal at each state. A system organised
> around the buyer's mind is far harder to copy than a collection of pages.

## 1. The conversion thesis

```
ATTENTION  →  RECOGNITION  →  TRUST  →  PARTICIPATION  →  CONVERSATION  →  GROWTH OPS
 (TikTok,     ("that's       (evidence +   (diagnostic /   (WhatsApp /     (qualified
  search,      exactly my     honest        tool / input)   consultation)   lead → paid
  pain)        business")     confidence)                                   engagement)
```

Each stage has one job and one failure mode. The strategy is to move the visitor one stage at a
time, never skipping — most agency sites jump from attention straight to "book a call" and lose
everyone in between.

| Stage | The job | The failure mode to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Attention | Earn the first 3 seconds | Generic hero that could be any agency |
| Recognition | Name their exact problem | Vague "we grow sales" claims |
| Trust | Show *how we know* | Claiming intelligence without demonstrating it |
| Participation | Make trying feel free + easy | Forms, gates, friction, upload anxiety |
| Conversation | Make talking feel safe | "Vague consultation call" dread |
| Growth Ops | Frame the paid offer clearly | Hiding the real service behind free tools |

## 2. Who the visitor is

The site speaks to commerce operators, not marketers:

- Business owners & founders
- Ecommerce managers · marketplace managers
- Restaurant / delivery-app operators
- Sales managers · commercial directors
- Social commerce & WhatsApp sellers

They are **busy, sceptical, and impatient.** They scan; they don't read. They have been burned
by agencies. They respond to precision and operational fluency, and they instantly detect
marketing fluff.

---

## 3. What the visitor feels (and what the site does about it)

| They feel… | The site must… |
|---|---|
| Confused why sales aren't growing | Name the likely leak precisely (pattern recognition) |
| Sceptical of agencies | Demonstrate, not claim; show evidence + confidence |
| Tired of generic advice | Be specific to their market, channel, and pattern |
| Afraid of wasting time | Show value in seconds; make every step short |
| Unsure which channel is the problem | Map their channels; localise the leak |
| Worried competitors are winning | Name the competitive pattern, calmly and concretely |
| Unclear whether growth is profitable | Separate revenue from profit explicitly (the H1 heuristic) |
| Impatient | Front-load insight; defer asks |

---

## 4. The two emotional jobs

Every page does two things at once:

1. **Reduce friction & fear:**
   - cognitive load · form fatigue · agency distrust · upload anxiety · uncertainty about what
     they'll receive · fear of a vague consultation call.
2. **Create pull:**
   - curiosity · self-recognition · trust · confidence · desire to test the diagnostic ·
     willingness to share a link/file · willingness to talk to Growmerce.

If a UX choice doesn't either reduce a fear or create pull, it doesn't belong on the page.

## 5. The experience principles (the house style of the UX)

These derive directly from the Intelligence Brain's operator heuristics (20) and the Pattern
Engine's recognition goal (19).

### P1 — Show intelligence before asking for anything
Insight is the magnet. The visitor must receive a real, specific observation about *their* kind
of business before any email field appears. Value precedes capture, always.

### P2 — Recognition over explanation
Don't explain what Growmerce does; make the visitor recognise themselves. A named pattern
("your discount increases orders but destroys contribution margin") does more than a paragraph
of service description.

### P3 — Evidence-aware, honestly confident
Mirror the Brain's Trust layer (20): every insight carries *how we'd know* and *how sure we
are.* "We strongly suspect, here's how we'd confirm" outperforms false certainty and is a
core differentiator from generic AI audit sites.

### P4 — Operator language, never marketing language
Talk like someone who has run a marketplace listing, a delivery menu, a WhatsApp inbox. Concrete
nouns (buy-box, contribution margin, response flow), not abstractions (synergy, optimisation,
solutions).

### P5 — Input feels like intelligence, not paperwork
The structured input is a Spotlight/Raycast command experience — suggestions, editable chips,
progressive disclosure — not a Typeform. (See `Structured_Input_Experience.md`.)

### P6 — Outcomes, not features
Tools, services, and pages are organised by the outcome the operator wants (find revenue leaks,
improve marketplace performance), never by internal taxonomy.

### P7 — Mobile-first, TikTok-speed
The default visitor is on a phone, arriving from a 30-second video. The flow must deliver a
recognisable insight within a few thumb-scrolls. (See `Mobile_TikTok_Landing_Experience.md`.)

### P8 — One obvious next step per surface
Every surface drives one primary action (diagnose / use tool / WhatsApp). Never a wall of equal
choices. Secondary actions are visibly secondary.

### P9 — The free thing proves the paid thing
Free tools/diagnostics/reports are proof of expertise that make Growth Operations the obvious
next step — they are framed as a taste of the engine, not as the destination.

### P10 — Respect the operator's intelligence
No dumbing down, no hype, no fake urgency. Treat the visitor as a smart, busy professional. The
absence of marketing manipulation is itself a trust signal to this audience.

## 6. The conversion architecture (free → paid)

```
FREE (proof of expertise)                         PAID (the actual product)
─────────────────────────                         ─────────────────────────
Pattern recognition content        ┐
Outcome-organised tools            ├─►  capture  ─►  Growth Operations
Commerce diagnostic + report preview┘   (lead)        (10/11/12)
                                                       via WhatsApp / consultation
```

The site never hides the paid service, and never lets the free assets feel like the whole offer.
The free layer demonstrates the Brain; the paid layer *is* the Brain doing the work.

---

## 7. Success measures for the UX (what "good" means)

The UX is working when, against real traffic:
- Visitors reach a **recognition moment** (a named pattern) before any ask.
- Diagnostic / tool **starts** are high relative to landing visits.
- **Capture** happens *after* value is shown, with low abandonment.
- **WhatsApp conversations** start with the visitor already feeling understood.
- Qualified **Growth Operations** opportunities are produced, not just newsletter signups.

These tie directly to the website goal: turning attention, pain, and curiosity into diagnostics,
conversations, and Growth Operations opportunities.

---

## 8. What this strategy explicitly rejects

- Generic agency navigation (Home/Services/About/Contact).
- Logo walls and unearned social proof.
- Early gates and long forms.
- Feature lists and service catalogues.
- Hype, fake urgency, and vague slogans.
- Claiming intelligence the page doesn't demonstrate.

Every page document that follows is an application of sections 4–6 above. If a page violates a
principle, the page is wrong — not the principle.
