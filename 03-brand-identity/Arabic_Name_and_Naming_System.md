# Arabic Name & Naming System

> How the name is written, read, and used — across English, Arabic, and bilingual contexts. The
> name is the most-used element of the identity; its rules are foundational. Growmerce is
> **Arabic-first**, so the Arabic form is not a translation but a primary, crafted asset.

---

## 1. The names

- **English / Latin:** **Growmerce** — a portmanteau of *grow* + *commerce*. It encodes the
  promise (growth) and the domain (commerce) without an arrow or a cart.
- **Arabic:** **جرومرس** — the transliteration of Growmerce.

Both are the same brand; neither is subordinate in its own context. In Arabic surfaces the Arabic
form leads; in global/Latin surfaces the Latin form leads; the design system makes both feel
equally crafted.

---

## 2. Arabic readability considerations

The Arabic wordmark **جرومرس** must be evaluated and crafted for:

- **Clarity of the transliteration** — it should read naturally as "Growmerce" to an Arabic
  reader, with comfortable letter connections and proportions.
- **The "g" sound** — rendered with **ج** (the common, widely-understood choice across most Arabic
  markets). Note dialect variation in how ج is pronounced; **ج is the recommended default**, but
  Claude Design / a native typographer should confirm it reads cleanly and is the strongest choice
  for the target markets (see Open Questions).
- **Letterform craft** — the joins (ج‑ر‑و‑م‑ر‑س) must be drawn with premium care; this is a core
  seriousness signal, not a font default.
- **Balance with the Latin wordmark** — the two should share a sense of weight, rhythm, and
  seriousness so the bilingual lockup feels unified.

> The Arabic wordmark is a **designed asset**, not a system-font rendering. Getting **جرومرس** to
> feel premium and natural is one of the most important deliverables of the visual phase.

---

## 3. Usage rules by context

| Context | Primary form | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Arabic website / UI** | Arabic **جرومرس** | Arabic leads; Latin name may appear secondarily where natural |
| **English / global** | Latin **Growmerce** | Latin leads; Arabic may appear in bilingual lockup |
| **Bilingual surfaces** | defined lockup | both forms, balanced; Arabic-first ordering in RTL contexts |
| **Reports** | matches document language | Arabic reports lead Arabic; keep the name consistent within a document |
| **Website (general)** | Arabic-first | RTL-native; Latin name for the brand mark and platform names |
| **Social (TikTok/Instagram/WhatsApp)** | handle in Latin (**growmerce**), display name bilingual | platform handles are typically Latin; display/profile can show both |

**Consistency rule:** within any single surface or document, the name is rendered consistently —
don't mix Arabic and Latin forms of the *brand name* arbitrarily in the same context (platform
names are a separate matter — they stay in their native Latin form).

---

## 4. The Arabic descriptor (positioning line)

Growmerce needs a short Arabic descriptor that states the category for an Arabic audience. The
brief's candidate:

- **شركة تشغيل نمو المبيعات الأونلاين** — "a company that operates online sales growth."

This is accurate and clear but long. Directions to refine (with a native commerce writer — see
`21-brand-narrative-system/Arabic` guidance and Open Questions):

- Shorter forms emphasising **تشغيل نمو المبيعات** ("operating sales growth") — the operational
  core.
- Forms that carry **الذكاء التجاري** ("commerce intelligence") — the engine.
- A pairing: a primary short descriptor + a one-line explanatory subline.

**Indicative options to explore (not final):**
- "تشغيل نمو المبيعات أونلاين" (operating online sales growth — tighter)
- "نمو المبيعات أونلاين، مدفوعًا بالذكاء التجاري" (online sales growth, powered by commerce
  intelligence — mirrors the English positioning)

> Do not finalise the descriptor here unless it becomes obviously right; lock it with a native
> commerce writer during the narrative-terminology work (shared with `21`). The descriptor must
> use operator Fusha and obey the narrative vocabulary/guardrails (no hype, no cliché).

---

## 5. The descriptor's relationship to positioning

The Arabic descriptor must carry the same dual positioning as the English:

```
English:  Online Sales Growth Operations  ·  powered by  ·  Commerce Intelligence
Arabic:   تشغيل نمو المبيعات أونلاين        ·  مدفوعًا بـ   ·  الذكاء التجاري
```

Both halves matter: **operations** (what we do) and **intelligence** (how). The descriptor should
not collapse into just "marketing" (ماركتنج) or just "consulting" (استشارات) — both would
misposition the brand.

---

## 6. What to avoid in naming

- Translating the brand name itself into a descriptive Arabic phrase (the name stays **جرومرس**).
- A weak, system-font Arabic wordmark that looks like an afterthought.
- Mixing Latin and Arabic name forms inconsistently within one surface.
- A descriptor that sounds like an agency ("وكالة تسويق") or generic consultancy.
- Over-formal/literary Arabic in the descriptor (operator Fusha only — per `21`).

---

## 7. Deliverables for Claude Design (naming)

- A crafted **Arabic wordmark جرومرس** (premium, natural, balanced with the Latin).
- A crafted **Latin wordmark Growmerce**.
- A defined **bilingual lockup** with ordering rules per context.
- A recommended **Arabic descriptor** (refined with a native writer) and its usage rules.
- Small-size and monochrome behaviour for both wordmarks.

> The name is where Arabic-first becomes real. If **جرومرس** is crafted with the same care as the
> Latin wordmark — and reads naturally and premium to an Arabic operator — the brand's core stance
> is proven in the most-seen element of the whole identity.
