# 07 — Occasion & Demand Knowledge

> What Growmerce knows about *when* commerce happens — seasonality, occasions, timing, and the cycles
> of demand. Timing is the dimension that changes the meaning of every other signal: the same
> conversion rate, the same stockout, the same price is good or bad depending on where it sits in the
> demand cycle. This is the doctrine of demand over time.

Organised by knowledge-object type (03).

---

## 1. Principles (the durable truths)

- **Demand is rhythmic, not flat.** Most categories have predictable cycles — seasonal, occasion-
  driven, weekly, daily. Treating demand as constant misreads every signal taken at a point in time.
- **Predictable demand events are operational deadlines, not surprises.** A peak season or gifting
  occasion is known in advance; the only question is whether the business prepared on the lead-time
  schedule.
- **The early window of a peak is the most valuable and most often missed.** Early, high-intent demand
  converts at full price; businesses that prepare *during* the peak forfeit it and recover it later
  only via discounting.
- **Timing changes the buyer, not just the volume.** During gifting occasions the *buyer and the user
  differ*; intent, needs, and reassurance requirements change, not merely the quantity.
- **A demand spike is only revenue if operations can serve it.** Demand the business can't fulfil
  (stockouts, capacity) is demand handed to competitors (links to Operations, 02).
- **Every peak is an acquisition event, not just a sales event.** A surge of new customers is the
  hardest, most expensive cohort to win; failing to convert them into a base wastes the acquisition.

## 2. Models (how demand moves over time)

### The demand-curve model
Each occasion/season has a characteristic curve: a build-up (early high-intent), a peak, and a
decline. Preparation must be back-planned from the *onset of the build-up*, accounting for stock and
creative lead times — not timed to the peak date.

### The occasion-readiness model
```
captured peak value  =  demand curve  ×  readiness (stock, creative, offer, experience) at each point
```
Value lost = the area under the demand curve where readiness was missing. Most loss is in the early
window (unprepared) and at stockout points (unavailable).

### The buyer-shift model (occasion-specific intent)
An occasion can change *who* is buying and *why*:
- **Gifting occasions** — buyer ≠ user; needs are confidence ("will they like it?"), convenience
  (wrap, delivery-by-date), and reassurance (easy exchange).
- **Self-purchase peaks** — deal-intent rises; comparison intensifies.
The same product must be merchandised and supported differently by occasion.

### The post-peak model
After a peak, demand cliffs. The strategic question is whether the surge cohort is converted into a
recurring base or allowed to vanish — determining whether each peak compounds the baseline or resets
it to zero.

## 3. Demand-cycle knowledge (the types of timing)

- **Seasonality** — broad, recurring annual cycles (weather, school, fiscal). The business's *own*
  historical curve outranks category seasonality where they differ.
- **Occasions / events** — discrete, dated demand events (holidays, cultural/religious occasions,
  gifting moments, paydays, platform sale events).
- **Demand cycles within periods** — weekly (weekday vs. weekend) and daily (mealtime peaks for
  delivery, evening for retail) rhythms.
- **Intent shifts** — the *kind* of demand changes by timing (gifting vs. self-purchase; planned vs.
  impulse), not only the amount.
- **Demand spikes** — sudden surges (viral moment, event, weather), which may be unforecastable and
  test operational responsiveness rather than planning.
- **Demand declines** — predictable troughs (post-peak, off-season) requiring different tactics
  (retention, clearance, base-building) than peaks.

## 4. Market-shift knowledge (longer-horizon timing)

Beyond cycles, demand is reshaped by *shifts* — durable changes rather than rhythms:
- Category demand trending up or down (a knowledge input to whether "flat sales" is a leak or a market
  reality).
- Platform/algorithm/policy changes that alter how demand is captured (links to Marketplace, 04, and
  Failure Modes, 22).
- Competitive entry/exit reshaping available demand (links to Competitor, 08).
Distinguishing a *cycle* (will recover) from a *shift* (won't) is a critical interpretive judgement —
misreading a shift as a trough (or vice versa) drives the wrong strategy.

## 5. Benchmarks (interpreting demand signals in time)

- **Launch/preparation timing vs. the demand-curve onset** — campaigns/stock arriving after build-up
  has begun → early-window loss.
- **Peak-cohort second-purchase rate** — near-zero → the peak is being rented annually, not banked.
- **A metric vs. its seasonal expectation** — judge any signal against the *same point* in prior
  cycles, not against an annual average.

## 6. Heuristics (operator rules-of-thumb)

- Back-plan preparation from demand onset and lead times, not from the date.
- Capture the early window at full price before any discounting.
- Treat recurring occasions as scheduled operations, not surprises.
- During gifting peaks, design for the gifter (buyer ≠ user).
- Plan the post-peak retention play *before* the peak, not after.
- Always ask: is this a cycle (will recover) or a shift (won't)?

## 7. Anti-patterns

- Preparing for a peak during the peak.
- Reading a seasonal trough as a performance failure (or a seasonal peak as durable success).
- Treating a gifting occasion like a self-purchase occasion.
- Winning a peak cohort and letting it vanish (no post-peak retention).
- Confusing a structural market decline with a temporary dip.

## 8. Exceptions

- **Genuinely a-seasonal categories** — some demand is close to flat; forcing a seasonal lens
  misleads.
- **Deliberate late entry** (clearance/long-tail strategy) — entering after the peak can be
  intentional.
- **True novelty/viral spikes** — unforecastable; the discipline is responsiveness, not planning.

> Occasion and demand knowledge is the time dimension of all other domains: it tells the Brain whether
> a signal is good, bad, or merely seasonal, and whether "flat" means a leak or a cycle. The
> Occasion patterns of workstream 19 are this doctrine crystallised into diagnosable form (09).
