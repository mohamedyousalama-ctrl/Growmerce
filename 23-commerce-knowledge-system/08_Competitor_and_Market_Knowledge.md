# 08 — Competitor & Market Knowledge

> What Growmerce knows about competition and market structure — the context that determines whether a
> given tactic works at all. The same move (raise price, add a tier, push organic) succeeds or fails
> depending on the competitive dynamics and the maturity of the market. This is the doctrine of
> commercial context.

Organised by knowledge-object type (03). Two linked sub-domains: **Competitor** (the rivals) and
**Market** (the structure they compete in).

---

## 1. Principles (the durable truths)

- **Competitors leave clues.** Their pricing, reviews, content, assortment, and positioning reveal
  what's working and where the real battle is. The market is readable from the outside.
- **The dangerous competitor is the one compounding, not the one ahead.** A rival currently behind but
  building organic equity (reviews, rank, content, audience) is a worse long-run threat than one
  currently ahead but static. Trajectory beats snapshot.
- **Whoever owns the comparison frame owns the category.** The criteria buyers use to compare are
  often set by the leader and favour the leader. Competing inside that frame is a structural
  disadvantage.
- **Structural advantages can't be out-tactic'd.** A competitor with a real cost, scale, or
  integration advantage will win a price war; the response is to change the axis of competition, not
  to match.
- **Market structure determines which tactics work.** A move that wins in a fragmented, immature
  market fails in a saturated, mature one — and vice versa.
- **Differentiation is a position, not a feature.** It is a defensible axis on which you are the
  obvious choice for a segment — not a list of attributes.

## 2. Models (how competition and markets behave)

### The competitive-position model
A business's position is decided on a chosen axis (price, value, experience, niche, service, speed).
Strength = being the clear choice on an axis that *matters to a segment* and that rivals can't easily
claim. Weakness = competing on an axis a rival owns structurally.

### The comparison-frame model
Buyers evaluate against a set of criteria (the frame). The frame is contestable: introducing a new,
genuinely-relevant criterion you win on can reframe the category in your favour. Accepting the
incumbent's frame concedes the contest before it starts.

### The market-maturity model
```
EMERGING → GROWING → MATURING → SATURATED
```
- **Emerging/growing** — demand outpaces supply; growth comes from capturing new demand;
  differentiation matters less; speed and presence win.
- **Maturing/saturated** — supply outpaces demand; growth comes from *taking share* and from
  retention/efficiency; differentiation, positioning, and economics dominate. Acquisition costs rise;
  the durable winners compound equity.
Diagnosing maturity tells you whether to chase demand or take share — opposite strategies.

### The competitive-response model
Competitors react. A price cut invites matching (a race to the bottom if both can sustain it; a trap
if you can't). A differentiation move is harder to copy and less likely to trigger a destructive
response. Anticipating response is part of choosing a move.

## 3. Competitor knowledge (reading rivals)

- **Organic vs. paid posture** — a rival compounding organic equity has a falling acquisition cost
  over time; a paid-dependent rival has a rising one. This trajectory is the key competitive read.
- **Pricing posture** — distinguish a *tactical* price gap (promo, temporary) from a *structural* one
  (cost/scale advantage); the response differs entirely.
- **Frame ownership** — identify which comparison criteria the category uses and who they favour.
- **Assortment & content cadence** — reveal where a rival is investing and where they're exposed.
- **The real competitor set** — buyers' actual alternatives, which are often not who the business
  thinks they are.

## 4. Market knowledge (reading the structure)

- **Category maturity** — emerging vs. saturated (drives the chase-demand vs. take-share decision).
- **Market saturation** — how much unmet demand remains; high saturation makes acquisition expensive
  and retention/differentiation essential.
- **Market structure** — fragmented (many small players, room to differentiate) vs. concentrated (few
  dominant players, frame owned, hard to enter).
- **Differentiation space** — which axes are unclaimed and valued by a segment; where a defensible
  position is available.
- **Demand trajectory** — whether category demand is rising or falling (a knowledge input to whether
  "flat sales" is a leak or a market reality — links to Occasion & Demand, 07).

## 5. Benchmarks (interpreting competitive signals)

- **Share-of-search trajectory vs. competitors** — a rival's rising share at flat-yours signals
  compounding advantage.
- **Competitor review/rank velocity** — accruing faster than yours → equity gap widening.
- **Margin erosion correlated with price-matching** — evidence of an unwinnable structural price
  fight.
- **Buyer language vs. the frame** — buyer questions clustering on leader-favouring criteria → the
  frame is owned by a rival.

## 6. Heuristics (operator rules-of-thumb)

- Watch competitor *trajectory*, not just their current position.
- Before matching a price, determine whether the gap is tactical or structural.
- Don't fight on an axis a competitor owns; change the axis.
- In a saturated market, compete on retention and differentiation; in a growing one, on speed and
  presence.
- Identify the buyer's *actual* alternatives, not your assumed rivals.

## 7. Anti-patterns

- Reacting to a competitor's current snapshot while ignoring their trajectory.
- Reflexive price-matching against a structurally cheaper rival.
- Accepting the leader's comparison frame by default.
- Applying growth-market tactics in a saturated market (or vice versa).
- Defining the competitor set by ego (the brands you admire) rather than by the buyer's real choices.

## 8. Exceptions

- **You hold the structural advantage** — then price competition is appropriate, not a trap.
- **Genuinely undifferentiable commodity** — differentiation space may be near-zero; compete on
  cost/operations (confirm before assuming).
- **A frame that genuinely reflects what matters** — reframing would be dishonest; win on the real
  criterion instead.
