# 06 — Customer Behavior Knowledge

> What Growmerce knows about how customers actually behave — across discovery, evaluation, purchase,
> and repeat. Customer behaviour is the bedrock domain: conversion, retention, pricing perception, and
> competitive choice are all customer behaviour observed at different moments. Understanding the buyer
> is understanding the whole funnel.

Organised by knowledge-object type (03).

---

## 1. Principles (the durable truths)

- **Customers buy on intent, trust, and friction — not on cleverness.** A purchase happens when the
  offer is clear (intent matched), trust is sufficient, and the path is frictionless. Remove any one
  and the sale dies.
- **Trust must be built before the ask, not after.** A journey that demands commitment before earning
  confidence leaks high-intent buyers who leave "to think about it."
- **The first purchase is a trial, not a win.** The customer is deciding whether you're worth a second
  order; the post-purchase window decides lifetime value.
- **Buyers buy outcomes, not products.** They are hiring the product to do a job; they evaluate the
  result they want, not the attributes you list.
- **Decisions are made relatively and under uncertainty.** Buyers compare options against references
  and resolve uncertainty with proof; they rarely evaluate in the absolute.
- **Deferral is the default for considered purchases.** Without a reason to act now, interested buyers
  postpone — and most postponed purchases never happen.

## 2. Models (how buyers move)

### The buyer journey model
```
DISCOVERY → EVALUATION → PURCHASE → POST-PURCHASE → REPEAT → LOYALTY
```
Each stage has a distinct psychology and a distinct failure mode. The journey is not linear in
practice (buyers loop, leave, return), and it spans channels — discovery on one, purchase on another.

### The intent–trust–friction model (the conversion core)
```
conversion likelihood ≈ f(intent matched, trust sufficient, friction low)
```
At the commitment moment, the binding constraint is usually *trust* (for considered/new) or *friction*
(for decided buyers at checkout). Diagnosis means identifying which constraint binds.

### The trust-building model
Trust accumulates from proof placed *before* each commitment: social proof (reviews, ratings),
risk-reversal (guarantees, easy returns), and pre-empted objections (answering the buyer's real
question). Trust is built where the anxiety is, at the moment it peaks.

### The second-purchase model
Repeat is decided in the post-purchase window: did the customer realise the value (onboarding/usage),
and were they re-engaged at the natural reorder point? An empty post-purchase window forfeits the
hinge of LTV.

## 3. Behaviour by stage

### Discovery behaviour
- Buyers discover through the channels *they* use, not the ones the seller prefers.
- Discovery intent varies: search is high-intent and comparison-driven; social/feed is low-intent and
  interruption-driven; the same buyer behaves differently by channel.
- Buyers arriving from a hook expect continuity; a mismatch between the hook and the destination kills
  the momentum the hook created.

### Evaluation behaviour
- Buyers evaluate against a reference set and a comparison frame (often the category leader's).
- They resolve uncertainty with proof and seek answers to a small set of decisive questions ("will it
  fit me, will it work, can I return it").
- They scan before they read; the first impression (image, headline, price, proof) decides whether
  they engage further.

### Purchase behaviour
- The decision-to-buy and the act-of-buying are different; many decided buyers are lost to checkout
  friction (forced accounts, long forms, surprise costs, limited payment, poor mobile).
- Deal-intent and premium-intent buyers behave differently; bringing deal-seekers to a premium offer
  (or vice versa) produces bounce.

### Repeat & loyalty behaviour
- Repeat is often habit, convenience, or lock-in — fragile attachment that evaporates when a
  competitor removes the friction. *True* loyalty (preference, advocacy) is rarer and more durable.
- Churn clusters at predictable moments (post-honeymoon, missed reorder window, after a price increase
  or service failure), not randomly.
- A small high-value cohort typically drives a disproportionate share of profit; they behave — and
  should be treated — differently from casual buyers.

## 4. Consumer psychology knowledge

- **Relative judgement / anchoring** — value and price are judged against references (shared with
  Pricing, 05).
- **Loss aversion** — fear of a bad outcome (wrong fit, wasted money) blocks purchase; risk-reversal
  addresses it directly.
- **Social proof** — others' behaviour reduces perceived risk; recency of proof matters.
- **Cognitive load / friction aversion** — every extra step, field, or decision sheds buyers; clarity
  and simplicity convert.
- **Inertia / status-quo bias** — without a reason to act now, the default is to do nothing.

## 5. Behaviour across channels

The same psychology expresses differently by context:
- **Marketplace** — high-intent, comparison-driven, platform-mediated trust (links to 04).
- **Own store** — intent varies by source; trust must be built by the brand itself (no platform to
  borrow it from).
- **Delivery apps** — fast, hungry, visual decisions; merchandising and availability dominate.
- **Social / feed** — low initial intent, interruption-driven; the job is to *create* intent and
  carry it.
- **WhatsApp / conversational** — high-intent enquiries that die without a strong, fast response flow;
  the conversation *is* the funnel.

## 6. Benchmarks (interpreting behaviour signals)

- **Conversion vs. category/AOV/consideration norm** — a number is only "low" relative to the right
  benchmark; high-consideration/high-AOV converts lower by nature.
- **Second-purchase rate vs. the category's reorder window** — below norm → a post-purchase/onboarding
  gap, not necessarily a product problem.
- **Repeat with low advocacy** — fragile, friction-driven "loyalty," not preference.

## 7. Heuristics (operator rules-of-thumb)

- If engaged traffic doesn't convert, suspect trust or friction before price.
- Put the strongest proof at the moment of greatest anxiety (the commitment step).
- Treat the first order as the start of the relationship, not the finish line.
- Measure loyalty quality (advocacy), not just loyalty quantity (repeat).
- Match the experience to the *intent* of the traffic you bring.

## 8. Anti-patterns

- Asking for commitment before building trust.
- Celebrating first orders while ignoring the second-purchase rate.
- Mistaking habit/lock-in for loyalty.
- Treating all customers identically (under-serving the high-value, over-discounting them).
- A page built around the seller's story instead of the buyer's decisive questions.

## 9. Exceptions

- **Genuinely one-time-purchase categories** — the second-purchase doctrine shifts to referral/review,
  not repeat.
- **Pure-commodity, price-only buyers** — intent/trust/friction still apply, but differentiation is
  limited (confirm before assuming).
- **Impulse categories** — evaluation collapses; discovery and friction dominate over deliberation.
