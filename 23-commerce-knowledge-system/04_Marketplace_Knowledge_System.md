# 04 — Marketplace Knowledge System

> Everything Growmerce should know about marketplaces — as **transferable knowledge**, not
> platform-specific tactics. Amazon, Noon, and delivery marketplaces differ in detail but share a
> deep structure: they are economies with rules, where visibility is earned and rented, and where
> the mechanics reward velocity, recency, and relevance. This document is the doctrine of how
> marketplaces actually work.

Organised by knowledge-object type (03). The point is understanding that applies across any
marketplace, present or future.

---

## 1. Principles (the durable truths)

- **A marketplace is an economy with rules, not a billboard.** Visibility is allocated by mechanics,
  not bought by presence. You win by understanding and serving the mechanics.
- **The algorithm optimises for the marketplace's revenue, not yours.** It surfaces what converts and
  earns the platform money. Align with that and it carries you; fight it and it buries you.
- **Visibility is earned or rented, and rented visibility collapses.** Rank bought purely with ads or
  promotions disappears when the spend stops; earned rank (relevance + conversion + velocity)
  persists.
- **Velocity begets visibility begets velocity.** Sales velocity drives rank; rank drives impressions;
  impressions drive sales. This loop runs upward or downward — there is no stable middle.
- **Recency is a proxy for relevance.** Marketplaces weight recent signals (recent sales, recent
  reviews) because recency predicts current demand. Lifetime totals decay in influence.
- **The shelf is won in a half-second.** On a results page, the buyer scans image, title, price, and
  rating in under a second. Most of the competition is decided there, before the product page.

## 2. Models (how the parts relate)

### The marketplace performance model
A seller's marketplace outcome decomposes into four sequential stages, each a multiplier:
```
RELEVANCE → VISIBILITY (rank/impressions) → CLICK (results-page economics) → CONVERSION (listing) → (velocity feeds back to relevance)
```
A weakness at any stage caps everything downstream, and downstream weakness eventually erodes
upstream rank (low conversion → lower rank). Diagnosis means finding the weakest stage.

### The ranking-equity model
Rank is a stock of equity built from: relevance (keyword/catalogue match), conversion rate, sales
velocity, review quantity *and recency*, availability, and fulfilment quality. Equity is earned
slowly and lost quickly (a stockout or a conversion drop withdraws it fast). Ads/promotions can
*accelerate* equity-building but cannot *substitute* for it.

### Results-page (thumbnail) economics
The click is decided by the scan block: main image (hero benefit, contrast, clarity), title
(search-relevant benefit front-loaded), price presentation, rating, and badges (delivery speed,
best-seller). High impressions + low click-through means the scan block is losing — independent of
product quality.

## 3. Benchmarks (how to interpret marketplace signals)

(Directional reference values — exact thresholds are category- and platform-specific; the knowledge
is *what to compare and why*.)
- **Click-through vs. category norm** — below-benchmark CTR at healthy impressions → a scan-block
  (image/title/badge) problem, not a demand problem.
- **Review recency vs. competitors** — a healthy lifetime count with stalled recent velocity, while
  competitors accrue recent reviews, signals freshness loss even at higher totals.
- **Rank-to-support correlation** — if rank tracks ad spend/promo tightly and organic conversion is
  weak, the rank is rented.
- **Stockout days on hero SKUs** — each is direct lost revenue *and* a rank-equity withdrawal.

## 4. Heuristics (operator rules-of-thumb)

- Protect the velocity of your hero listings above all — it feeds everything.
- Fix the click before opening the traffic tap (more impressions into a weak scan block wastes
  demand).
- Treat reviews as a continuous flow, never a launch milestone.
- Never let a hero SKU stock out; the rank cost outlasts the lost sales.
- Earn rank before you scale spend; scaling spend on weak organic conversion is renting at a loss.

## 5. Marketplace economics knowledge

- **Commission/fee structures** compress margin and must be in the fully-loaded contribution (links to
  Profitability, 02). A "bestseller" can be a loss after commission + fulfilment + ad cost.
- **Sponsored placement** is a tax on visibility that can cannibalise organic sales you'd have won
  anyway — measure incremental, not gross, sponsored revenue.
- **Buy-box / featured-offer** mechanics concentrate sales; losing it (even on your own catalogue)
  silently cuts revenue.
- **Promotions** lift velocity (and thus rank) short-term but train discount-seeking and erode margin
  if habitual (links to Promotion, 05).

## 6. Behavioural knowledge (search & consumer behaviour on marketplaces)

- **Marketplace search is high-intent and comparison-driven.** Buyers arrive ready to compare and
  decide quickly; the results page is a comparison engine.
- **Buyers apply a "social-proof freshness" filter** — recent reviews reassure more than old ones,
  even at lower totals.
- **Buyers scan, they don't read** — the half-second scan block decides the click; the product page
  only gets a chance if the scan block wins.
- **Trust is platform-mediated** — buyers trust the marketplace's signals (ratings, badges,
  delivery promises) more than the seller's claims.

## 7. Competitive positioning knowledge

- The dangerous competitor is the one *compounding* review and rank equity, not the one currently
  ahead (links to Competitor, 08).
- The category leader often owns the comparison frame (the criteria buyers compare on); competing
  inside a leader-favouring frame is a structural disadvantage.
- Price wars against a structurally cheaper rival on a marketplace are unwinnable; differentiate the
  scan block and the value, not the price.

## 8. Anti-patterns (recurring marketplace mistakes)

- Buying rank with ads/promos while leaving organic conversion broken (renting an unsustainable
  position).
- Treating reviews as a one-time launch task (recency stalls, rank decays).
- Optimising the product page while losing the click on the results page.
- Reading a single day's rank as a trend.
- Scaling spend on a listing with weak organic conversion.

## 9. Exceptions (where the rules bend)

- **Deliberate scarcity / drop models** — stockouts may be strategic, not a leak.
- **Pure-paid categories** — some marketplaces/categories have little organic surface; the
  earned-vs-rented distinction weakens.
- **New launches** — temporary support to build initial velocity is legitimate equity-building, not
  renting, *if* organic conversion is improving underneath.

## 10. Why this knowledge is transferable

None of the above names a single platform's UI. Ranking-by-velocity-and-recency, the
visibility-velocity loop, results-page economics, earned-vs-rented rank, and platform-mediated trust
are true of Amazon, Noon, delivery apps, and marketplaces not yet built. That transferability is why
this is *knowledge*, not a platform tactic — and why it compounds across every marketplace engagement
Growmerce runs. The patterns of workstream 19 (Review Density Gap, Thumbnail Economics, Ranking
Durability) are this knowledge crystallised into diagnosable form (09).
