# 13 — Knowledge Gaps & Unknowns

> Critical. An institution's integrity rests as much on knowing what it does *not* know as on what it
> does. This document defines how Growmerce represents the boundaries of its knowledge, handles
> uncertainty, and — above all — prevents false certainty. Knowing the edge of your knowledge is
> itself a form of knowledge, and a rare one.

This is the knowledge-level expression of the same honesty the Brain enforces (calibrated abstention,
20) and the narrative promises (calibrated confidence, 21).

---

## 1. The principle

> Growmerce treats its own ignorance as first-class. Every domain has an edge; the system represents
> that edge explicitly, reasons about it, and never lets the absence of knowledge masquerade as
> confident knowledge.

The most dangerous failure of an intelligence institution is not being wrong — it is being
*confidently* wrong about something it never actually knew. The defences below exist to make that
structurally difficult.

---

## 2. The kinds of "not knowing"

Not all unknowns are the same; the system distinguishes them because they're handled differently:

| Type of unknown | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Known gap** | we know there's a thing we don't know | "we have no validated benchmark for this niche category" |
| **Provisional belief (assumption)** | we have a hypothesis, not validated knowledge | "this segment is probably price-sensitive — unconfirmed" |
| **Bounded knowledge (exception territory)** | a rule holds *except* in cases we've flagged | "this pattern's fix is unproven in saturated markets" |
| **Coverage gap (per business)** | knowledge exists but we lack the *evidence* to apply it here | "we know how to assess profit; we lack this business's cost data" |
| **Genuine uncertainty** | the matter is inherently uncertain or contested | "whether this demand dip is a cycle or a shift" |
| **Unknown unknown** | a gap we haven't yet recognised | (mitigated structurally — §6) |

Distinguishing these matters: a *known gap* prompts knowledge acquisition; a *coverage gap* prompts
evidence acquisition (22); an *assumption* must be flagged and never relied on as fact; a *genuine
uncertainty* must be surfaced as such.

---

## 3. How gaps are represented

Knowledge gaps are not silent absences; they are **explicit objects** in the system:
- A **domain's edges** are documented — each domain (02) records where its validated knowledge ends
  (thin areas, unproven playbooks, niches without benchmarks).
- **Assumptions are typed objects** (03) carrying a `provisional` status — they can inform reasoning
  but never as facts, and they are flagged wherever they're used.
- **Exceptions** (03) explicitly bound where knowledge does *not* apply.
- **Coverage** is tracked per business (22) — the system knows which signal categories it lacks for
  this engagement.

Because gaps are explicit, the Brain can *reason about them*: it can say "I can't conclude X without
knowledge/evidence Y," which is a conclusion, not a silence.

---

## 4. How uncertainty is handled

When knowledge is incomplete or provisional, the system:
1. **Down-weights** the affected reasoning (a conclusion resting on an assumption can't reach high
   certainty — links to Quality, 12, and the Source Hierarchy, 22).
2. **Surfaces** the uncertainty honestly rather than hiding it ("the evidence is consistent with two
   explanations; we can't yet distinguish them").
3. **Names the resolver** — what knowledge or evidence would remove the uncertainty ("a benchmark for
   this niche," "this business's cost data"), turning the unknown into a next step.
4. **Abstains** when the gap is decisive — "we can't determine this yet" is a valid, correct output
   (the Brain's calibrated abstention, 20).

---

## 5. How unknowns affect confidence

Gaps and uncertainty propagate directly into output confidence:

```
high-certainty, validated knowledge + full coverage      → high confidence permitted
provisional/assumption-based knowledge                   → confidence capped (often Medium)
known gap in required knowledge OR coverage gap          → abstain or low-confidence, with the gap named
contradicted/deprecated knowledge                        → conclusion not made
```

This is the upstream source of the brand's calibrated confidence: the customer-facing "we strongly
suspect, here's how we'd confirm" (21) is, underneath, the Knowledge System honestly accounting for
its own gaps. The honesty is *structural*, not stylistic.

---

## 6. Preventing false certainty (the structural defences)

False certainty is prevented by design, not goodwill:

1. **Assumptions can't become facts.** They are typed `provisional` and capped in authority (03, 12);
   no laundering an assumption into a confident claim.
2. **Confidence is computed from knowledge certainty + evidence**, not asserted (12 → 20/22). The
   system *cannot* express more confidence than its knowledge and evidence support.
3. **Counter-knowledge and exceptions are checked** before a conclusion (the "what would make this
   wrong" discipline — the Brain's GUARD, 20). A pattern is ruled out where an exception applies.
4. **Coverage is enforced** — the system won't be confident about a dimension it has no evidence for
   (22).
5. **Abstention is legitimate** — there is no pressure to produce an answer; "we don't know yet" is an
   acceptable institutional output.

For **unknown unknowns** (the gaps we haven't recognised), the structural mitigation is the
compounding/quality loop itself: outcomes that contradict expectations (11, 12) reveal gaps we didn't
know we had, converting unknown unknowns into known gaps over time. The institution discovers the
edges of its ignorance by being wrong in measured, captured ways — and learning from it.

---

## 7. Why representing unknowns is a competitive advantage

A system that always produces a confident answer is *less* trustworthy than one that knows its limits —
because the sophisticated operator has been burned by confident-but-wrong advice. By representing its
unknowns explicitly, Growmerce:
- earns trust the way a serious institution does (a credible rater states confidence and abstains when
  warranted);
- avoids the catastrophic, relationship-ending error of confident wrongness;
- creates honest next steps (acquire the missing knowledge/evidence) instead of bluffing;
- and turns its own ignorance into a roadmap for compounding (every known gap is a target for the
  knowledge-acquisition loop, 11).

> Knowing what you don't know is the mark of expertise, not its absence. Growmerce treats the
> boundaries of its knowledge as carefully as the knowledge itself — which is exactly why what it
> *does* claim to know can be trusted.
