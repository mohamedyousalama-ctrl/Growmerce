# 05 — Pricing & Promotion Knowledge

> What Growmerce knows about price and promotion — independent of any pattern or business. Pricing and
> promotion are where margin is most often silently destroyed and most often recoverable, because most
> pricing problems are *understanding* problems, not number problems. This is the doctrine of how
> price and promotion actually behave.

Organised by knowledge-object type (03).

---

## 1. Principles (the durable truths)

- **Price is perceived relatively, not absolutely.** Buyers judge a price against a reference; with no
  anchor, every price feels high. Price perception is constructed, not read off the number.
- **Value is communicated, not assumed.** A real value advantage that isn't communicated does not
  exist in the buyer's mind; they choose on what they can perceive.
- **A discount is a transfer of margin, justified only by incrementality.** A discount that doesn't
  generate a sale you wouldn't otherwise have made is pure margin given away.
- **Discounting trains behaviour.** Habitual discounts teach customers — including loyal ones — to
  wait for the next one, eroding full-price demand and deepening dependence.
- **Most pricing problems are not "the number is wrong."** They are communication, architecture
  (anchoring/tiering), or willingness-to-pay-segmentation problems.
- **The right battlefield is rarely price.** Competing on price against a structurally cheaper rival
  transfers your margin to the customer with no durable share gain.

## 2. Models (how price and promotion behave)

### The price-perception model
```
perceived value  ÷  perceived price (relative to anchor)  =  perceived deal
```
Both terms are constructed by framing. You can win the "deal" perception by raising perceived value
(communication) or by providing a higher reference anchor — without lowering the actual price.

### The incrementality model
```
promo value  =  (incremental units × incremental margin)  −  (baseline units × discount given away)
```
Most promotions are judged by *redemption volume* (units sold on promo), which is the wrong number.
The right number is *lift over baseline*. A high-redemption, low-lift promo is a margin loss wearing
the costume of a success.

### The willingness-to-pay (WTP) distribution model
A customer base has a spread of WTP. A single price under-monetises the high end (who'd pay more for
more) and loses volume at the low end (who'd buy a cheaper option). Tiering (good/better/best) captures
both ends; a single price is a compromise that captures the least.

### The fully-loaded margin model (shared with Profitability, 02)
Effective price after discount, minus *all* variable costs (COGS, shipping, fees, returns,
acquisition) = true contribution. Price and promotion decisions made on gross margin routinely produce
negative true contribution.

## 3. Pricing knowledge (the levers)

- **Anchoring** — a reference point (compare-at, cost-of-alternative, a higher tier) makes the target
  price legible; without one, the buyer defaults to "expensive."
- **Architecture / tiering** — good/better/best guides choice, captures WTP spread, and supports
  premium; the middle is usually the intended winner.
- **Psychological thresholds** — crossing a round-number boundary changes perception
  disproportionately; small price moves near thresholds have outsized effects.
- **Elasticity** — varies by category, brand strength, substitutability, and segment; commodity-like
  offers are elastic, differentiated/branded offers less so. Elasticity is *not* uniform across the
  base (see WTP model).
- **Competitive pricing** — match only where the gap is tactical; where it is structural, reframe
  value instead of matching (a losing fight, links to Competitor, 08).

## 4. Promotion knowledge (the mechanics)

- **Discount mechanics** — broad/always-on discounts mostly subsidise demand you already had;
  targeted discounts (first order, lapsed winback, gated actions) are more incremental.
- **Offer structures** — the *structure* of an offer (bundle, tier, guarantee, reason-to-act-now)
  often matters more than the price; a clearer offer beats a lower price.
- **Bundles** — bundles built around real *use logic / co-purchase* lift AOV and solve needs; bundles
  built to clear slow stock neither sell nor add value.
- **Promotion fatigue** — repeated promotion desensitises; each one delivers less lift and trains
  more waiting. Promotion is a depleting resource if overused.
- **Cannibalisation** — a promotion can pull forward demand (stealing future full-price sales) or
  shift volume between SKUs/channels without net gain; lift must be measured net of cannibalisation.
- **Reason-to-act-now** — honest urgency (deadline, limited bonus, genuine scarcity) converts existing
  intent that would otherwise defer; deferral is where most considered-purchase sales die.

## 5. Benchmarks (interpreting price/promo signals)

- **Discount penetration** (share of orders carrying a code) above category norm with falling blended
  margin → likely non-incremental bleed.
- **Full-price vs. promo demand** — a small gap between baseline and promo demand → low incrementality.
- **Price-objection rate vs. competitive position** — objections out of line with the actual market
  position → an anchoring/communication problem, not a price-level problem.

## 6. Heuristics (operator rules-of-thumb)

- Never judge a promotion by redemption; judge it by lift.
- Don't show discounts to customers who'd buy at full price.
- Fix value communication before cutting price.
- If "expensive" objections don't match your market position, you have an anchoring problem.
- One price for a mixed-WTP base leaves money on both ends.

## 7. Anti-patterns (recurring mistakes)

- **Discounting to fix a non-price problem** (masking weak trust, clarity, or conversion with a
  coupon).
- **Always-on promotion** (training the base to never pay full price).
- **Judging SKUs on gross margin** (ignoring fully-loaded contribution).
- **Bundling to clear stock** (instead of around use logic).
- **Matching a structurally cheaper competitor's price** (transferring margin with no share gain).
- **Anchorless single pricing** (every price feels high; no choice architecture).

## 8. Exceptions (where the rules bend)

- **Deliberate, funded loss-leaders** with a profitable attach/LTV — a "negative-margin" item can be
  rational acquisition.
- **Penetration pricing** as a deliberate, time-boxed strategy with a defined exit.
- **Genuinely commodity categories** where price *is* the deciding factor and differentiation is
  unavailable — then price competition is appropriate (rare, and worth confirming before assuming).

> Pricing and promotion knowledge is high-leverage because it moves *profit* fastest and because the
> errors are so common and so invisible. The patterns of workstream 19 (Price Advantage Not
> Communicated, Anchorless Pricing, Discounting Buys Sales Anyway, Bundling By Convenience) are this
> doctrine crystallised into diagnosable form.
