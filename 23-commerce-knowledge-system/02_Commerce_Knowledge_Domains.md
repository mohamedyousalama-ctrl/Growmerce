# 02 — Commerce Knowledge Domains

> The major divisions of commercial understanding. Domains are how the Knowledge System is organised
> at the top level — the "departments" of Growmerce's institutional expertise. Each domain is a
> coherent body of transferable knowledge with its own facts, principles, models, benchmarks, and
> playbooks (03).

---

## 1. Why organise by domain

Knowledge that isn't organised can't be reasoned with, retrieved, validated, or grown. Domains give
the Knowledge System structure the way practice areas give a professional firm structure: each is a
recognisable area of expertise, deep enough to matter, distinct enough to maintain, and connected to
the others through the shared ontology (14).

Domains are organised by **the nature of the commercial reality they describe**, not by industry or
platform — because knowledge organised by industry doesn't transfer (the fashion-marketplace lesson
stays in the fashion folder), whereas knowledge organised by commercial reality applies everywhere.
(This mirrors the patterns-over-playbooks logic of 19.)

---

## 2. The domain structure

Growmerce's knowledge is organised into **sixteen domains**, grouped into four families.

### Family A — Channel & format knowledge (where commerce happens)
| Domain | Contains | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Marketplace Knowledge** | ranking, visibility, reviews, buy-box, marketplace economics, search & consumer behaviour on platforms | most sellers' demand is governed by mechanics they don't understand; transferable across Amazon, Noon, etc. (04) |
| **Delivery Knowledge** | menu-as-storefront mechanics, availability, timing, merchandising, delivery economics | delivery platforms run on rules distinct from web; menu/availability govern revenue |
| **Ecommerce Knowledge** | store funnels, conversion, checkout, on-site trust, catalogue, lifecycle | the operation after the click — where most value is won or lost |
| **Retail Knowledge** | assortment, merchandising, inventory, omni-channel, physical-digital interplay | grounds digital knowledge in commercial fundamentals |

### Family B — Commercial-mechanics knowledge (how value is captured)
| Domain | Contains | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Pricing Knowledge** | price psychology, anchoring, tiering, elasticity, competitive pricing, margin effects | most pricing problems are understanding problems; pricing moves profit fastest (05) |
| **Promotion Knowledge** | discount mechanics, incrementality, offer structures, bundles, promotion fatigue, cannibalisation | discounting is where margin silently leaks (05) |
| **Profitability Knowledge** | fully-loaded contribution, unit economics, cost structure, revenue-vs-profit, cash | the discipline that separates Growmerce from revenue-chasers (the H1 doctrine) |
| **Growth Knowledge** | constraints, leverage, compounding vs. rented growth, sequencing, acquisition vs. retention economics | what actually moves the number, and in what order |

### Family C — Demand & behaviour knowledge (who buys and why)
| Domain | Contains | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer Behavior Knowledge** | discovery, evaluation, purchase, repeat, trust, friction, decision psychology | customers act on intent/trust/friction; the root of conversion & retention (06) |
| **Conversion Knowledge** | the path from intent to purchase, friction, objection, the commitment moment | where qualified demand is converted or lost |
| **Retention Knowledge** | repeat cadence, churn timing, loyalty vs. lock-in, lifecycle, LTV | the second purchase is where economics work; most underinvested area |
| **Occasion & Demand Knowledge** | seasonality, occasions, demand cycles, timing, intent shifts | timing changes the meaning of every other signal (07) |

### Family D — Competitive & operational knowledge (the context and the doing)
| Domain | Contains | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Competitor Knowledge** | competitive dynamics, positioning, comparison frames, competitive response | competitors leave clues; the dangerous one is compounding (08) |
| **Market Knowledge** | market structure, category maturity, saturation, differentiation | the same tactic works or fails depending on market context (08) |
| **Channel Knowledge** | channel mix, attribution, concentration risk, create-vs-capture, channel-customer fit | where demand comes from and how durable it is |
| **Operations Knowledge** | availability, fulfilment, returns, response flow, lead times, operational constraints | most sales problems are operational; the unglamorous source of growth |

## 3. What each domain holds (the internal structure)

Every domain is structured the same way (a consequence of the Knowledge Objects Framework, 03), so
the system is navigable and maintainable:

```
DOMAIN
 ├── Facts            stable, verifiable truths in this domain
 ├── Principles       durable causal truths
 ├── Models           explanatory/causal structures
 ├── Frameworks       structured ways to analyse the domain
 ├── Heuristics       operator rules-of-thumb
 ├── Benchmarks       reference values for interpreting signals here
 ├── Patterns (19)    the recurring diagnosable realities of this domain
 ├── Anti-patterns    recurring mistakes / things that look right but fail
 ├── Exceptions       known cases where a rule does not hold
 └── Lessons          knowledge earned from outcomes in this domain
```

This common internal structure is what lets the Brain (20) and Supply Chain (22) reach into any
domain and retrieve the right kind of object for the reasoning step at hand (a benchmark to interpret
a signal, a model to explain it, a playbook to fix it).

This document is the index; documents `04`–`08` develop five of the most important domains in depth as
worked examples of the structure. The remaining domains follow the same template (and are listed for
build in `Backlog.md`).

## 4. How domains relate (no domain is an island)

Domains are connected through the ontology (14) and through real commercial causality. A few of the
most important cross-domain relationships:

- **Pricing × Promotion × Profitability** — a discount (Promotion) changes the effective price
  (Pricing) which changes contribution (Profitability). Reasoning about any one requires the others.
- **Customer Behavior × Conversion × Retention** — conversion and retention are customer behaviour
  observed at different lifecycle stages; the same psychology underlies all three.
- **Marketplace × Competitor × Pricing** — marketplace position is decided by competitive dynamics
  and pricing within the platform's economics.
- **Occasion × Demand × Operations** — a demand spike (Occasion) is only captured if operations
  (availability, fulfilment) are ready for it.

Cross-domain reasoning is where the deepest insight lives — the *compound* findings of the Brain (20)
are usually cross-domain. The domain structure organises knowledge; the ontology (14) connects it.

---

## 5. The doctrine of domains

- **Transferable, not industry-bound.** Domains describe commercial realities, so knowledge moves
  across industries and platforms. (The opposite of agency knowledge, which is trapped in verticals.)
- **Deep enough to matter.** Each domain is real expertise, not a topic heading — depth is the point.
- **Maintained, not static.** Each domain is owned, validated (12), and grown (11) over time.
- **Connected, not siloed.** The richest understanding is cross-domain, enabled by the ontology.

> The domains are the map of what Growmerce knows. Their depth is what makes the recommendations good;
> their transferability is what makes the knowledge compound; their structure is what makes it an
> institution rather than a collection of opinions.
